# Duct Sealing in NC Attics: What You're Losing and How to Fix It

Most Triangle homeowners assume their AC is the reason their electric bill spikes in summer. The system is working — the air handler is running, the vents are blowing — so the assumption is that running the AC more must cost more. That's true, but it misses a large part of the problem. A significant portion of the cooled air your system produces never reaches your living space at all. It leaks out before it gets there.

Here's what's happening inside your attic ducts and what it costs you.

---

## The Short Answer

According to the [U.S. Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/ducts), duct leakage in a typical home accounts for 25–40% of the energy used for heating and cooling. [Energy Star](https://www.energystar.gov/campaign/seal_insulate/identify_problems_you_want_fix/drafty_house/duct_sealing) estimates that about 20–30% of conditioned air is lost through leaks, holes, and poorly connected sections of ductwork before it reaches the rooms it's meant to serve.

In most NC Triangle homes, those ducts run through the attic — a space that reaches 130–150°F on a summer afternoon. You're paying to cool air to 55°F, then sending it through leaky flex duct surrounded by 140°F air. What arrives at your supply vent is warmer, less pressurized, and reduced in volume. Sealing those leaks is one of the highest-return energy improvements available in an older NC home.

---

## Why Attic Ducts Are a Bigger Problem in NC

Not every home has this issue. Homes where ducts run through conditioned space — inside the building envelope — lose far less because even leaking air stays inside. But in NC, the dominant construction pattern for homes built before 2010 is central forced air with duct runs through the attic. A [survey referenced by NREL and Appalachian State University research](https://www.nrel.gov/docs/fy03osti/33795.pdf) found that over 60% of NC ductwork is located in unconditioned attic space.

That placement is the problem. In Climate Zone 3A — NC's classification under the DOE building energy standards — summer attic temperatures routinely run 50°F or more above outdoor air temperature. Flex duct is insulated (typically R-6 or R-8), but it was never designed to function inside a 145°F space for four to five months a year.

Two things happen simultaneously:

**Heat gain through duct walls.** Even sealed, insulated ducts absorb heat from the surrounding attic air. The longer the duct run and the hotter the attic, the more heat is transferred into the airstream before it reaches the supply vent.

**Air loss through leaks.** Connections at boots, trunks, and flex duct joints degrade over time. Mastic sealant cracks. Metal tape peels. In older homes, some connections were never properly sealed to begin with. The air that leaks out goes directly into the attic — unconditioned space — and is wasted entirely.

---

## Duct Sealing vs. Duct Insulation: Different Problems

These are often confused, and addressing only one while ignoring the other leaves money on the table.

| Issue | Problem | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Duct leaks | Air escapes before reaching living space | Duct sealing (mastic or metal tape) |
| Duct heat gain | Conditioned air absorbs heat from attic | Duct insulation (R-8 minimum for NC) |
| Both | Common in homes built before 2000 | Seal first, then assess insulation level |

Sealing should come before adding insulation wrap. There's no point in insulating a leaky duct — you'd be trapping the leak inside the insulation and making it harder to address later.

Professional duct sealing uses mastic — a thick, flexible compound that's brushed or applied over joints and connections and remains pliable as ducts expand and contract with temperature changes. Metal foil tape (not standard duct tape, which fails in high-heat environments) is used for accessible seams. For more severe leaks, Aeroseal — an aerosol sealant injected through the duct system — can seal leaks from the inside without requiring full access to every run.

## What Duct Sealing Costs in NC

Costs vary based on system size, accessibility, and the extent of leakage. For a typical 1,500–2,500 sq ft Triangle home:

| Service | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Basic duct sealing (accessible joints) | $300–$600 |
| Full duct sealing (mastic throughout) | $600–$1,200 |
| Aeroseal pressurized injection | $1,500–$2,500 |
| Duct insulation upgrade (R-6 to R-8) | $800–$2,000 |

Before spending on full Aeroseal, a blower door or duct blaster test can quantify how much leakage you actually have. Homes with moderate leakage often see the best return from targeted mastic sealing at known problem areas — boots, trunk connections, and the air handler plenum.

## 2026 Rebates Available for NC Homeowners

This is worth knowing before you schedule any work. Two programs stack with duct sealing and insulation projects:

**Duke Energy Smart Saver Program**
Duke Energy offers rebates to NC customers for qualifying home efficiency upgrades. As of 2026, duct sealing and insulation work is eligible for rebates up to $450 through the [Duke Energy Smart Saver program](https://www.duke-energy.com/home/products/home-energy-improvement). Rebates are paid after project completion and require a participating contractor or documentation of the work performed.

**Energy Saver NC (State Program)**
The [Energy Saver NC program](https://energysavenc.org), available across all 100 NC counties, provides up to $1,600 for insulation and air sealing work for eligible households. Income qualifications apply for some tiers, but the program has broad eligibility. Work must be performed by a certified contractor listed in their directory.

These programs can meaningfully reduce the net cost of duct sealing — especially when combined with related attic work.

## How Duct Sealing Works With a Radiant Barrier

Duct sealing and radiant barrier installation address different parts of the same problem and work best together.

A [radiant barrier](/blog/what-is-a-radiant-barrier) reduces the attic air temperature by reflecting up to 97% of radiant heat from the roof deck before it can warm the attic space. When attic temperatures drop by 20–30°F, the heat gain through duct walls decreases proportionally — meaning your sealed, insulated ducts are operating in a cooler environment than they would without the barrier.

The Florida Solar Energy Center documented that homes with HVAC ducts in the attic see 15–17% cooling cost reductions from radiant barrier installation — specifically because of reduced duct heat gain. Combine that with sealed ducts that aren't leaking 20–30% of airflow, and you've addressed both the quantity and the temperature of air reaching your living space.

If your home has attic ducts and you haven't addressed either issue, the combination is where the most significant efficiency gains are available. See [Is a Radiant Barrier Worth It in North Carolina?](/blog/is-radiant-barrier-worth-it-north-carolina) for a full breakdown of the economics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know if my ducts are leaking?**
Signs include rooms that never reach the thermostat setting, uneven temperatures across floors, high summer electric bills despite a functioning AC, and visible disconnected sections if you can access your attic. A duct blaster test (performed by an energy auditor or HVAC technician) gives a precise measurement of total leakage.

**Is duct tape okay for sealing ducts?**
Standard fabric duct tape fails in attic conditions. It dries out, shrinks, and peels off within a few years of exposure to high heat. Proper duct sealing uses mastic compound or metal foil tape (UL 181-rated), both of which hold up in high-temperature attic environments long-term.

**Does duct sealing actually make a noticeable difference?**
Yes — especially in older homes with significant leakage. Homeowners who seal ducts in homes with previously heavy leakage commonly report that rooms previously underserved by the system (far from the air handler, upper floors) become noticeably more comfortable. The AC also cycles off sooner because it reaches the thermostat set point faster.

**Can I seal ducts myself?**
Accessible connections at the air handler plenum and visible trunk branches can be DIY-sealed with mastic or metal foil tape. Flex duct joints buried in insulation or in tight attic corners are harder to access safely. For a complete job, particularly before applying for Duke Energy or Energy Saver NC rebates that require contractor documentation, a professional assessment is the right starting point.

**What's the payback period for duct sealing?**
At $600–$1,200 installed and annual savings of $80–$200 depending on leakage severity and home size, payback runs 4–10 years before rebates. After stacking Duke Energy and Energy Saver NC programs, net cost can drop significantly, shortening payback to 2–6 years. Duct sealing also extends AC equipment life by reducing runtime — a harder-to-quantify but real benefit.
